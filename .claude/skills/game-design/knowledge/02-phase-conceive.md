# Phase 1: CONCEIVE — Knowledge Reference

Generate a raw concept worth pursuing. Apply mobile-first thinking from the start.

---

## Appeal Taxonomy (Jonas Tyroller)

Every successful concept has at least one strong pre-play attraction:

- **Fantasy appeal**: "I want to *be* this" — power fantasy, role, identity
- **Exploration appeal**: "I want to *explore* this" — world, system, mystery
- **Toy appeal**: "I want to *touch* this" — satisfying interaction, tactile feel

On mobile: **Toy appeal dominates casual**. The mechanic must feel good in 3 taps.
Fantasy appeal drives mid-core retention (character identity, role).

---

## Hook + Anchor Framework (Ryan Clark / Chris Zukowski)

Every concept needs both:

- **Hook**: one sentence that makes someone want to *discuss* the game
  ("What if the world ended every 3 days and you had to decide who to save?")
- **Anchor**: a familiar mechanic that makes it feel safe to try
  ("It's Match-3 but you're matching memories in a time-loop")

**Too innovative is a real failure mode**: Arco was critically praised and sold badly.
A hook without an anchor drives curiosity but not downloads.

On mobile: the anchor must be immediately recognizable in the **first screenshot**.

---

## Genre DNA Technique

Strip any genre to its atomic mechanic, then swap the skin entirely:
- FPS (align crosshair with target) + camera = Pokemon Snap
- FPS + pressure washer = PowerWash Simulator
- Tower defense stripped from RTS → entire genre
- Backpack inventory stripped from dungeon crawlers → Backpack Hero

**Remove a core mechanic**: opens as much design space as adding one.
Captain Toad can't jump → transforms platformer into puzzle.

**Mobile application**: identify the single satisfying verb of your game.
If you can't name it in 3 words, the concept isn't ready.

---

## Vibe Doc over GDD

FTL's entire design spec was one page describing how the game would **feel**:
"Captain Picard yelling at engineers" — no genre, no mechanics, no structure.

Write your concept as a vibe doc first:
- What does the player feel moment-to-moment?
- What is the satisfying micro-loop (the thing they do 100 times)?
- What is the emotional arc over a full session?

Skip the GDD until Phase 3 (DESIGN). Premature structure = premature lock-in.

---

## Four-Part Concept Test

Before calling something a "game concept" answer all four:
1. What is the **win state**? (or session end condition on mobile)
2. What **obstacle** pushes the player away from it?
3. What is the **fail state**?
4. What **actions** does the player take to overcome the obstacle?

If you can't answer all four, you have a theme or aesthetic, not a game.

---

## Mobile Conceive Filters (apply before moving on)

**One-thumb test**: Can the core verb be performed with one thumb in portrait mode?
If no: is landscape justified? Does it require both hands? Is that acceptable for your genre?

**Session fit test**: Can a satisfying micro-session complete in under 5 minutes?
If no: is this mid-core? Does your target audience accept longer sessions?

**Interruption test**: Can the game be paused and resumed mid-session without penalty?
Mobile players get interrupted constantly. Design for this from day one.

**Novelty vs. familiar test**: Is there a comparable game already in the top charts?
- If yes → what is your ONE differentiator? (not three; one)
- If no → is the concept too far from what players know? Apply Hook+Anchor

## Flappy Bird Pattern

Simple premise + disproportionately brutal learning curve = viral potential.
The gap between **apparent simplicity** and **actual difficulty** is itself the hook.

On mobile: the frustration-to-retry loop must be under 10 seconds.
Rage games, one-more-try mechanics, and mastery games all exploit this.

---

## Story Generator Frame (Tynan / RimWorld)

Instead of designing a game, design a **story generator**:
Study the stories players tell about existing games (Reddit, reviews, TikTok).
Emergent player narratives — not mechanics — reveal what to build toward.

Mobile application: look at 1-star reviews of competitor games.
Players describe the stories they expected but didn't get. That's your design brief.
